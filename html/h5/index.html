The Heading Elements (<h1>–<h6>)

As defined in the HTML Living Standard, these elements represent headings for their sections. The rank of a heading is given by the number in its name (1 for the highest rank, 6 for the lowest).

Hierarchy and Navigation

Headings should represent the outline of your document. Screen reader users often navigate pages by jumping from heading to heading to find the content they need.

  • One H1 per Page: Best practice is to have exactly one <h1> that describes the primary purpose of the page.
  • Don't Skip Levels: Do not jump from an <h2> directly to an <h4>. This breaks the structural map of the page.
  • Meaning, Not Style: Choose heading levels based on their structural rank, not their visual size. Use CSS to change font sizes if needed.

WCAG Requirement: Info and Relationships

Success Criterion 1.3.1 (Level A): Information, structure, and relationships conveyed through presentation can be programmatically determined.

Headings are the most important landmarks on a page. When they are used correctly, they programmatically define the nesting and hierarchy of information. This allows assistive technology to build a "Table of Contents" on the fly for the user.

Headings as Labels

Headings often serve as labels for the sections that follow them. Clear, descriptive headings help users with cognitive disabilities understand the page's organization.

WCAG Requirement: Headings and Labels

Success Criterion 2.4.6 (Level AA): Headings and labels describe topic or purpose.

Ensure your heading text is concise but descriptive. A heading like "Introduction" is less helpful than "Introduction to Semantic HTML" in a complex document.
